Abstract

Background

Key populations, including female sex workers (FSWs), men who have sex with men (MSM), people who inject drugs (PWID), and transgender individuals, bear a disproportionate burden of HIV infection. Improving outcomes for these populations requires understanding where the HIV care and prevention cascades are succeeding and where they are failing. Large-scale programmes serving key populations increasingly collect the routine data needed to answer these questions, but much of this data remains underutilized because health systems in the most affected countries lack sufficient analytical capacity [verify and cite]. Strengthening the HIV response therefore depends not only on generating programmatic evidence, but on building the data science workforce that can produce it.

Objectives

This thesis addresses both needs. The first two studies analyse routine programmatic data from Nigeria’s National HIV Key Populations programme, examining (a) factors associated with viral load testing and suppression among HIV-positive female sex workers initiating antiretroviral therapy, and (b) oral PrEP continuation rates and associated factors among key populations. Such analyses require data science skills that remain scarce in the countries most affected by HIV. The third study therefore evaluates the flagship course of The Graph Courses, an online training platform developed to strengthen these skills among health professionals in low- and middle-income countries.

Methods

All three studies employed retrospective analyses of routinely collected data. The first two analyzed programmatic data from Nigeria’s KP CARE 1 programme using mixed-effects logistic regression with random intercepts for service delivery facilities and multiple imputation by chained equations for missing data. The third analyzed administrative enrollment rosters and post-course survey responses from nine cohorts of an online R programming bootcamp, using logistic regression for quantitative outcomes and thematic coding for open-ended survey responses.

Results

Among 34,976 FSWs initiating ART between 2016 and 2022, 97.1% received at least one viral load test and 94.5% achieved viral suppression, with the likelihood of both outcomes improving over time. Advanced clinical stage at baseline was associated with lower odds of suppression, underscoring the importance of early diagnosis. Among 43,788 key population clients initiating oral PrEP between 2020 and 2023, the 6-month continuation rate was only 11.5%, revealing a critical gap on the prevention side of the cascade. Transgender individuals had the lowest continuation rate (3.5%), while female sex workers had the highest (13.7%). Across nine cohorts of the R Basics and Beyond bootcamp, over half of enrolled health professionals completed the course, with high satisfaction; both pre-recorded video lessons and live workshop interaction were highly valued, while time constraints were the primary barrier.

Conclusions

Taken together, the three studies illustrate a cycle in which data science skills enable the generation of programmatic evidence, and that evidence in turn identifies where the HIV response must be strengthened. The treatment cascade for FSWs in Nigeria is performing well but depends on early diagnosis; the prevention cascade requires urgent attention, particularly for transgender individuals and younger clients. Scaling access to the analytical skills demonstrated in these studies, as the training programme aims to do, is essential for sustaining data-driven improvements in HIV programmes and in global health more broadly.

General Introduction

The Global Burden of HIV and the Disproportionate Impact on Sub-Saharan Africa

Over four decades since its initial discovery, the human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) remains one of the most pressing global health challenges. In 2024, an estimated 1.3 million people newly acquired HIV, and 630,000 died from causes related to acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS), which is caused by the virus (1). Although annual new infections are now down by over 60% since their peak in 1995 (1), HIV infection is chronic and lifelong, meaning that every person who acquires the virus and survives adds to the prevalent pool over time. As such, the global number of people living with HIV has continued to rise, from an estimated 26.6 million in 2000 to 40.8 million in 2024 (1). Overall, HIV/AIDS remains the fourth-largest infectious disease contributor to global disability-adjusted life-year (DALY) losses, after lower respiratory infections, diarrhoeal diseases, and malaria (2,3).

Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA) suffers a disproportionate share of this burden. Of the 40.8 million people worldwide living with HIV in 2024, 26.3 million (65%) are in sub-Saharan Africa, although the region contains only about 15% of the world’s population (4). During the peak of the epidemic in the late 1990s and early 2000s, AIDS became the leading cause of adult mortality in the region (5). By 2005, adult HIV prevalence exceeded 1% in 37 of the 48 sub-Saharan African countries, and the worst-affected countries saw multi-decade declines in life expectancy (4). In Eswatini, for example, adult prevalence reached 27% and life expectancy dropped from 61 to 32 years (4,6), while in Botswana, prevalence peaked at 25% and life expectancy dropped from 65 to roughly 47 years (4,6).

Since that time, significant progress has been made against the epidemic, largely driven by the scale-up of HIV antiretroviral therapy (ART) across the region. Modern ART regimens, typically taken as daily pill formulations of multiple drugs, suppress viral replication to undetectable levels, preventing progression to AIDS and rendering the individual effectively unable to transmit HIV (7). The number of people receiving ART in SSA grew from approximately 5 million in 2010 to about 22 million in 2024. Over that same period, new HIV infections in SSA fell by 55%, from 1.46 million to 650,000, and AIDS-related deaths fell by 60%, from 940,000 to 380,000 (4). Despite this progress, the region continues to bear a disproportionate share of the global burden: 65% of all people living with HIV, 50% of new infections, and 60% of AIDS-related deaths (4).

Key Populations in the HIV Epidemic

Key populations are groups of people who, due to social or behavioral factors, have a higher risk and prevalence of HIV. These include sex workers, men who have sex with men (MSM), people who inject drugs (PWID), and people in prisons and other closed settings (8). For example, recent multicountry estimates put the risk of acquiring HIV at 23 times higher for MSM, 14 times higher for PWID, and 8.9 times higher for sex workers, compared with the general adult population (9). Added together, KPs and their sex partners accounted for 55% of new adult HIV infections in 2022 (9).

Beyond elevated acquisition risk, key populations living with HIV often experience worse outcomes along the care cascade. Across settings, key populations face pervasive stigma, discrimination, and criminalization that threaten their safety and undermine their rights (10), and many report high levels of violence, with over half experiencing sexual, physical, psychological, or economic violence in some contexts (11,12). These structural barriers have direct clinical consequences: co-morbid mental health conditions can undermine health-seeking behaviour, reduce treatment adherence, and increase mortality risk (13,14). Mental health conditions may also elevate HIV risk while limiting access to prevention, testing, and treatment (15), yet such conditions frequently go undiagnosed and untreated in low-resource settings, where integration of mental health into HIV services remains uncommon (16,17). Together, these factors contribute to delayed diagnosis, weaker retention in care, and lower rates of viral suppression among key populations.

Given this disproportionate burden, targeted and population-specific HIV prevention and treatment programmes are essential. Effective responses include comprehensive packages of HIV prevention, sexually transmitted infection (STI) screening and treatment, HIV testing, and referral to ART, alongside legal and social support for those experiencing violence. Differentiated service delivery models, such as community-based clinics that co-locate prevention, testing, and treatment services for key populations under one roof, are designed to reduce barriers and improve engagement across the cascade of care. Because key populations play a disproportionate role in onward transmission, successfully engaging them across the treatment cascade is essential to reducing HIV transmission at a population level (18). Community-based and peer-led approaches, including outreach, condom promotion, HIV testing, ART linkage, and PrEP rollout, are central to improving access and outcomes, particularly when services are safe, non-discriminatory, and responsive to the specific needs of key populations.

Key Populations in Nigeria and the Programmatic Response

Nigeria, the most populous country in Africa with over 220 million inhabitants, has the fourth-largest HIV epidemic globally (19,20). While the overall adult HIV prevalence is relatively low at approximately 1.3% (21), the epidemic is severely concentrated among key populations. The 2020 Integrated Biological and Behavioural Surveillance Survey (IBBSS) reported HIV prevalence rates of 16% among FSWs and 25% among MSM in several Nigerian states, far exceeding the general population rate (22). These disparities are compounded by a hostile legal environment: Nigeria’s Same-Sex Marriage Prohibition Act of 2014 criminalized homosexuality with sentences of up to 14 years imprisonment, and sex work exists in a grey legal zone where “living off the proceeds of prostitution” is criminalized (23). Such laws drive key populations underground, fuel stigma and discrimination in healthcare settings, and create barriers to accessing HIV prevention, testing, and treatment services (23,24).

In response to the concentrated HIV burden among key populations, a dedicated Key Populations programme was established in Nigeria with support from the United States President’s Emergency Plan for AIDS Relief (PEPFAR) and the United States Agency for International Development (USAID). The programme, implemented by Heartland Alliance LTD/GTE and other non-governmental organizations, operates across seven Nigerian states (Akwa Ibom, Bayelsa, Cross River, Jigawa, Lagos, Niger, and Yobe) and provides a comprehensive package of HIV prevention, testing, care, and treatment services to FSWs, MSM, PWID, and transgender individuals.

Service delivery is organized around strategically located “One-Stop Shops” (OSS), which are community-based clinics that provide a range of services under one roof, including HIV testing and counselling, ART initiation and refills, pre-exposure prophylaxis (PrEP), sexually transmitted infection screening and treatment, and other ancillary services. This model is designed to be accessible and KP-friendly, reducing the stigma and discrimination that key populations frequently encounter in mainstream healthcare facilities [verify and cite]. The programme also employs venue-based sampling and peer outreach strategies to engage key populations at hotspots such as brothels, bars, clubs, and other gathering places, delivering prevention services and facilitating linkage to care.

The KP programme uses an electronic data management system to track services provided to enrolled clients. This system generates a rich source of routine programmatic data that can be leveraged for research and programme improvement. The first two studies in this thesis draw on these data to examine critical outcomes along the HIV care and prevention cascade.

The HIV Care Cascade: Viral Load Testing and Suppression

The concept of the HIV care cascade, also referred to as the continuum of care, describes the sequential stages through which people living with HIV must progress to achieve optimal health outcomes: from diagnosis, to linkage to care, to ART initiation, to adherence and retention in care, and ultimately to sustained viral suppression [Gardner et al., 2011: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21332264/]. Each transition in the cascade represents a potential point of attrition, and understanding the factors that promote or impede progression through these stages is essential for strengthening the overall HIV response.

Viral suppression, defined as achieving and maintaining an undetectable or very low viral load (typically < 1000 copies/mL), is the ultimate goal of ART. Individuals who achieve viral suppression experience dramatically better health outcomes and, critically, have effectively zero risk of transmitting HIV to sexual partners, a concept captured by the widely promoted “Undetectable = Untransmittable” (U=U) message [verify and cite: Cohen et al. 2016]. The UNAIDS 95-95-95 targets call for 95% of people living with HIV to know their status, 95% of those diagnosed to receive ART, and 95% of those on ART to achieve viral suppression, placing viral suppression at the apex of the care cascade framework.

For female sex workers, achieving viral suppression is particularly consequential given their high number of sexual partners and consequent potential for onward transmission. However, FSWs face unique barriers that may impede their progression through the care cascade, including stigma and discrimination in healthcare settings, criminalization of sex work, high mobility, substance use, mental health challenges, and competing economic priorities [verify and cite: Shannon et al. 2015; Lancaster et al. 2016]. Despite the importance of understanding these barriers, relatively few large-scale studies have examined factors associated with viral load testing and suppression specifically among FSWs in sub-Saharan Africa. Most existing evidence on the FSW care cascade comes from small-scale studies or qualitative research, and large programmatic datasets have been underutilized for this purpose [verify and cite: Nnko et al. 2019].

The first study in this thesis addresses this gap by analyzing routine programmatic data from nearly 35,000 FSWs enrolled in the Nigerian KP programme to identify factors associated with receipt of viral load testing and achievement of viral suppression.

HIV Prevention: Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis and the Challenge of Continuation

While ART and viral suppression address the treatment side of the HIV response, pre-exposure prophylaxis (PrEP) represents a cornerstone of HIV prevention. PrEP involves the use of antiretroviral medications by HIV-negative individuals to reduce the risk of acquiring HIV infection. Randomized controlled trials have demonstrated that daily oral PrEP with tenofovir-based regimens can reduce HIV acquisition risk by up to 92% when taken consistently [verify and cite: Grant et al. 2010; McCormack et al. 2016].

Despite its proven efficacy, PrEP’s real-world effectiveness is substantially limited by challenges in uptake and continuation. A global systematic review found that approximately 70% of PrEP users across studies had discontinued treatment or shown suboptimal adherence within 6 months of initiation [verify and cite: Zhang et al. 2022]. Factors contributing to poor continuation include side effects, social stigma, logistical challenges in accessing refills, and fluctuating perceptions of HIV risk [verify and cite: Pillay et al. 2020]. These challenges are likely amplified among key populations in resource-limited settings, where structural barriers to healthcare access are compounded by criminalization and discrimination.

In Nigeria, PrEP rollout for key populations is a critical component of the national HIV prevention strategy [verify and cite: NACA 2022]. The KP CARE 1 programme has been actively providing oral PrEP to eligible KP clients, using a provider-facilitated approach in which individuals testing HIV-negative are offered PrEP as part of a comprehensive prevention package. However, prior to the second study in this thesis, data on PrEP continuation rates among key populations in Nigeria were very limited, hindering evidence-based programme improvement.

The second study in this thesis analyzes data from over 43,000 KP clients who initiated PrEP through the Nigerian KP programme, making it one of the largest PrEP continuation studies among key populations in sub-Saharan Africa. It examines 6-month continuation rates and identifies factors associated with sustained PrEP use across different KP groups.

Bridging HIV Research and Data Science Capacity

The analyses presented in the first two studies of this thesis, which identified factors associated with viral load testing, viral suppression, and PrEP continuation using large-scale programmatic data, required substantial data science skills. These included proficiency in statistical programming (specifically R), data cleaning and manipulation of large routine datasets, mixed-effects regression modelling, multiple imputation for missing data, and effective data visualization.

Such skills are increasingly essential across the global health workforce. The digitization of health information systems, from electronic health records to outbreak surveillance platforms to routine programme monitoring tools, has generated vast quantities of data with the potential to inform clinical and public health decision-making [verify and cite: Rajamani et al. 2025]. Yet the capacity to analyze these data remains critically limited, particularly in low- and middle-income countries. Surveys of public health professionals consistently reveal significant skill gaps in data analysis, with much health data remaining underutilized due to limited analytical capacity in health institutions [verify and cite: Kiosia et al. 2024]. The WHO African Regional Office has identified the training of health workers in data analysis as a priority for strengthening public health systems on the continent [verify and cite: Bonkoungou et al. 2025].

As a native of Nigeria who observed these capacity gaps firsthand, addressing the shortage of data analysis skills among health professionals in Africa was a core motivation for this doctoral research. The practical experience of building analytical pipelines for the HIV cascade studies reinforced the importance of making these skills widely accessible.

Approaches to Data Science Training

Multiple approaches have been used to strengthen data science skills in the health workforce. Short in-person workshops remain a popular format, delivering intensive instruction with hands-on practice and real-time feedback [verify and cite: Forsetlund et al. 2009]. International health organizations have long favored this model for its ability to build competencies rapidly in targeted groups. However, in-person workshops are difficult to scale: they are constrained by costs, geographical limitations, instructor availability, and the time required for organization and delivery [verify and cite: Bonkoungou et al. 2025].

Massive open online courses (MOOCs), which gained prominence in the early 2010s, offered a potential solution by providing access to educational content from world-class institutions at minimal cost to an unlimited number of participants [verify and cite: Pappano 2012]. However, MOOCs have been plagued by notoriously low completion rates, often in the single digits [verify and cite: Jordan 2014; Khalil & Ebner 2014]. Without the accountability structures, instructor interaction, and peer support that characterize in-person training, many online learners struggle to self-regulate their learning, leading to disengagement and dropout [verify and cite: Kizilcec et al. 2017; Reich & Ruipérez-Valiente 2019].

These challenges are likely amplified for programming and data science training. Learning to code requires not just understanding syntax but developing problem-solving and debugging skills that are difficult to acquire without guidance. R, the programming language used for all three studies in this thesis, is powerful and free but has a steep learning curve, particularly for users without prior programming experience [verify and cite]. Neither purely self-paced online courses (which leave learners stranded when they encounter errors) nor intensive in-person workshops (which may not provide sufficient time for sustained practice) are ideally suited to building programming fluency.

Cohort-based online courses represent a promising middle ground. These programmes typically use a flipped classroom model: learners engage with asynchronous content (pre-recorded videos, readings, exercises) on their own schedule, then participate in scheduled live sessions for instructor-led problem-solving and peer interaction [verify and cite: Bishop & Verleger 2013]. The asynchronous component provides flexibility and reduces total contact hours, while the synchronous component provides accountability, community, and direct support, all of which are often absent from purely self-paced courses [verify and cite: Shapiro et al. 2017]. The normalization of videoconferencing during the COVID-19 pandemic has created new opportunities to deliver such programmes at scale, even to learners in geographically dispersed settings [verify and cite: Dhawan 2020].

The GRAPH Network and The Graph Courses

The GRAPH Network (Global Research and Analyses for Public Health) was established in 2020 as part of a collaboration between the University of Geneva’s Institute of Global Health and the WHO African Regional Office to support the sub-Saharan African COVID-19 response [verify and cite: Botero-Mesa et al. 2022]. Initially focused on producing rapid analytical outputs for public health decision-makers, the network evolved to address a deeper underlying challenge: the shortage of data analysis capacity among health professionals in the region.

Out of this recognition, The Graph Courses (thegraphcourses.org) was developed as a dedicated online training platform for data science and AI in health and life sciences. The platform is headquartered at the University of Geneva and is legally housed under ASCRES (Association de Soutien aux Centres de Recherche, Enseignement et Soins en milieux à ressources réduites). Its flagship programme, the R Basics and Beyond (RBB) bootcamp, is a 12-week part-time online course that introduces R programming and data analysis to researchers and practitioners in health-related fields. The course uses a flipped classroom model: each week, students complete 2–3 hours of self-paced prework (pre-recorded video lessons, handouts, and quizzes), followed by a 2-hour live workshop featuring collaborative small-group problem-solving and instructor guidance.

The third study in this thesis evaluates the educational outcomes of the RBB programme across nine cohorts from 2023 to 2025, using enrollment data and post-course surveys to assess completion rates, participant satisfaction, and the experience of learners.

Thesis Objectives

This thesis has three specific objectives:

1.  To examine factors associated with viral load testing and viral suppression among HIV-positive female sex workers initiating antiretroviral therapy in Nigeria (Paper 1). Using routine programmatic data from nearly 35,000 FSWs enrolled in the National HIV KP programme, this study identifies demographic, clinical, and structural factors associated with receipt of viral load testing and achievement of viral suppression.

2.  To investigate oral PrEP continuation rates and associated factors among key populations in Nigeria (Paper 2). Using data from over 43,000 KP clients who initiated oral PrEP through the KP CARE 1 programme, this study examines 6-month continuation rates across different KP groups and identifies factors associated with sustained PrEP use.

3.  To evaluate the educational outcomes of a cohort-based online R programming course for health professionals (Paper 3). Using enrollment rosters and post-course survey data from nine cohorts of the R Basics and Beyond bootcamp, this study assesses completion rates, participant satisfaction, highlights, and challenges.

Additional Research During the Doctoral Period

In addition to the three core thesis papers, this doctoral period also produced a first-authored publication in Nature Communications on SARS-CoV-2 antibody seroprevalence in Yaoundé, Cameroon (Nwosu et al. 2021). That study, which originated from the candidate’s Master’s thesis project, estimated the prevalence of anti-SARS-CoV-2 IgG antibodies in a random sample of residents in a health district of Yaoundé and found an adjusted seroprevalence of 29.2%, approximately 322 times greater than the attack rate implied by official case counts at the time. The study highlighted the extent of unreported SARS-CoV-2 circulation in sub-Saharan Africa during the early pandemic period. While not directly related to the thesis themes, this work contributed to the broader research mission of the doctoral period and demonstrates the application of epidemiological and data analysis skills across different public health domains.

Methodological Contributions

Overview

The candidate played a central role in all three studies comprising this thesis: leading the data analysis and manuscript preparation for the HIV-related studies (Papers 1 and 2), and founding and leading the development of the training platform and evaluation study (Paper 3). This section details the candidate’s specific contributions to each study.

Paper 1: Viral Load Testing and Suppression Among FSWs

For the first study, the candidate led the statistical analysis, which involved processing and cleaning routine programmatic data from over 34,000 individuals, implementing mixed-effects logistic regression models with random intercepts for 16 care facilities, and conducting multiple imputation by chained equations (25 imputed datasets) for missing covariates. The candidate produced all figures and tables, wrote the original manuscript draft, and coordinated revisions with co-authors through to publication in PLoS ONE. The candidate also contributed to the study design and methodology, in close collaboration with the PhD supervisor and senior co-authors.

Paper 2: PrEP Continuation Among Key Populations

For the second study, the candidate again led the data analysis and manuscript writing. This involved analysing data from over 43,000 PrEP clients, implementing mixed-effects logistic regression with random intercepts for service delivery sites, and applying MICE with 10 imputations. The candidate designed the analytical approach, coordinated with the Heartland Alliance monitoring and evaluation team on data quality checks (deduplication, logic checks for date and categorical variables), and wrote the original manuscript draft. The candidate coordinated the revision process through to publication in BMJ Global Health.

Paper 3: Evaluation of the R Basics and Beyond Bootcamp

For the third study, the candidate’s role extended far beyond the analysis. As co-founder of The Graph Courses, the candidate led the development of the RBB curriculum, the online learning platform, and the instructional delivery over approximately one year of initial development, coordinating a team of more than 10 contributors including instructors, teaching assistants, and content developers. The candidate also served as lead author of the accompanying open-access course textbooks (Introduction to Data Analysis with R, available at datawithr.com, and Introduction to Data Science with Python, available at pythondatabook.com). For the evaluation study itself, the candidate designed the post-course survey instrument, led the data analysis (including the logistic regression of completion predictors and LLM-assisted qualitative coding of open-ended responses), and wrote the original manuscript draft.

Common Methodological Threads

All three studies share a common analytical philosophy: the use of routinely collected data, whether from health programme registers or educational administration systems, to generate actionable evidence for programme improvement. Papers 1 and 2 employed a consistent statistical framework of mixed-effects logistic regression with facility-level random intercepts and multiple imputation, allowing for the identification of individual-level predictors while accounting for facility-level clustering and missing data. Paper 3 employed a similar regression approach for completion predictors and complemented it with qualitative thematic analysis. All analyses were performed using R statistical software, and the code and summarized data underlying the published papers have been made publicly available.

Conclusions and Perspectives

Summary of Key Findings

This thesis set out to generate programmatic evidence for the HIV response among key populations in Nigeria and to evaluate a scalable approach for building the analytical capacity on which such evidence depends. The three studies, while spanning different domains, are linked by a common logic: that routine health programme data, when analysed with appropriate methods, can reveal where interventions are succeeding or failing, but only if the workforce has the skills to perform such analyses.

The first study examined viral load testing and suppression among nearly 35,000 HIV-positive female sex workers in Nigeria and found encouragingly high rates of both testing (97.1%) and suppression (94.5%). These figures compare favourably with overall viral suppression rates in Nigeria’s general HIV programme, which stood at approximately 80% in the same period [verify and cite: UNAIDS Nigeria country data]. The temporal trend, with increasing odds of both testing and suppression for those initiating ART in more recent years, reflects the maturation of the KP programme over time, including expanded viral load testing infrastructure and improved service delivery. The study also identified that FSWs presenting with more advanced HIV disease at baseline had significantly lower odds of achieving viral suppression, underscoring the importance of early diagnosis and treatment initiation.

The second study, analyzing PrEP continuation among over 43,000 key population clients, revealed a starkly different picture. The 6-month continuation rate of 11.5% indicates that the vast majority of individuals who initiate PrEP discontinue within the first six months. This rate is lower than comparable estimates from Cameroon (28%) and Zambia (25%) [verify and cite: Ndenkeh et al. 2022; Claassen et al. 2021]. The particularly low continuation rate among transgender individuals (3.5%) raises serious concerns and likely reflects the compounded effects of stigma, discrimination, and healthcare avoidance. Age and employment status emerged as additional predictors, with younger and unemployed clients less likely to continue PrEP, suggesting that economic insecurity may disrupt sustained engagement.

The third study demonstrated that a cohort-based online R programming course can achieve meaningful completion rates among health professionals. Over half of enrolled students completed the 12-week programme, substantially exceeding the single-digit completion rates typical of self-paced MOOCs [verify and cite: Jordan 2014]. Pre-recorded video lessons and live workshop facilitator interaction were both highly valued, supporting the pedagogical logic of the flipped classroom model. Time constraints and competing work priorities emerged as the dominant barriers to completion, consistent with the broader literature on adult online learning [verify and cite: Shapiro et al. 2017].

The HIV Cascade for Key Populations: Treatment Success, Prevention Gap

Juxtaposing the findings from the first two studies reveals a striking asymmetry in the Nigerian KP programme’s performance. On the treatment side, the programme has achieved remarkable success: once FSWs are diagnosed and linked to care, nearly all receive viral load testing and the vast majority achieve viral suppression. This demonstrates that community-based, KP-friendly service delivery models can overcome many of the structural barriers that typically impede care cascade progression for marginalized populations.

On the prevention side, the picture is far less encouraging. The very low PrEP continuation rates suggest that while the provider-facilitated approach is effective at promoting initial uptake, it has not succeeded in translating that uptake into sustained use. This disconnect echoes patterns seen in other PrEP programmes globally [verify and cite: Zhang et al. 2022] and points to a fundamental challenge: the factors that drive individuals to start PrEP may be quite different from those required to maintain it over time.

This asymmetry has important implications. Viral suppression among those already living with HIV is necessary but not sufficient for epidemic control. Without effective prevention among HIV-negative key population members, new infections will continue to sustain the epidemic. Closing the prevention gap, particularly by improving PrEP continuation, is therefore essential for achieving the broader goal of HIV epidemic control among key populations in Nigeria.

From Evidence to Capacity: Connecting the Three Studies

These programmatic insights were only possible because the data existed and because someone had the skills to analyse it. The KP CARE 1 programme’s electronic register contains records on tens of thousands of clients, but those records do not speak for themselves. Turning them into the evidence presented in Papers 1 and 2 (involving mixed-effects logistic regression with facility-level random intercepts, multiple imputation for missing data, forest plots, and choropleth maps) required specific data science competencies: proficiency in R, familiarity with regression modelling and missing data methods, and the ability to translate statistical output into actionable programme recommendations.

These are precisely the competencies that the R Basics and Beyond bootcamp was designed to build. The course teaches health professionals to clean, manipulate, and visualize data in R, and to interpret statistical output. These are the foundational skills for the type of analysis demonstrated in Papers 1 and 2. In this sense, the three studies form a single arc: the first two show what is possible when analytical capacity meets programmatic data; the third addresses how to make that capacity more widely available, particularly among health professionals in the low- and middle-income countries where such data are most abundant and least utilized [verify and cite: Kiosia et al. 2024].

Implications for HIV Treatment and Viral Suppression

The high rates of viral load testing and suppression observed in the first study are encouraging, but several findings warrant attention for continued programme improvement. First, the association between advanced WHO clinical stage at baseline and lower odds of viral suppression reinforces the well-established finding that late diagnosis compromises treatment outcomes [verify and cite: Kitahata et al. 2009; Boyd et al. 2019]. This is particularly concerning given the barriers to early testing among FSWs, including fear of disclosure, stigma, and distrust of healthcare providers. Strategies to promote earlier diagnosis, such as community-based testing, self-testing, and integration of HIV testing with other services used by FSWs, should be prioritized [verify and cite].

Second, the association between education level and viral load testing adherence, though modest in magnitude, highlights the potential value of health literacy interventions for FSWs with limited formal education. Programmes might consider integrating simplified health education materials and adherence support into routine care, particularly for clients with lower educational attainment.

Third, the steady improvement in both testing and suppression rates over the study period suggests that the programme’s quality improvement efforts have been effective. Identifying and documenting the specific programmatic changes that drove these improvements, such as expansion of viral load testing capacity, transition to more effective ART regimens (notably the shift from efavirenz- to dolutegravir-based regimens), and refinement of community-based service delivery, would be valuable for guiding similar programmes in other settings [verify and cite].

Addressing the PrEP Continuation Crisis

The very low PrEP continuation rates observed in the second study demand urgent attention. Several strategies merit consideration.

First, the provider-facilitated approach to PrEP initiation, while effective at driving uptake, may inadvertently enrol individuals who are not fully ready to commit to sustained use. Incorporating more thorough readiness assessments and motivational counselling at initiation could help identify and support clients who are most likely to benefit from sustained PrEP use [verify and cite].

Second, the stark disparities across KP groups call for group-specific interventions. Transgender individuals, for example, had continuation rates nearly four times lower than FSWs. For transgender individuals, addressing the intersecting challenges of stigma, discrimination, competing health priorities (such as gender-affirming care), and healthcare avoidance may require dedicated service delivery strategies, including transgender-competent providers and peer support [verify and cite: Teng et al. 2023].

Third, the association between unemployment and lower PrEP continuation suggests that economic barriers, including transport costs, lost wages, and pharmacy fees, may disrupt the routines required for sustained PrEP use. Programme adaptations such as multi-month dispensing, mobile or community-based refill options, and integration of PrEP services with economic support programmes could help address these barriers [verify and cite].

Fourth, it is important to recognize that low continuation does not necessarily equate to programme failure. Key populations often experience fluctuating HIV risk, and some clients may appropriately cycle on and off PrEP in response to changing risk behaviours [verify and cite: Reed et al. 2021; Fazito et al. 2012]. Future research should distinguish between discontinuation due to poor adherence and intentional, risk-informed cessation, as these have very different programmatic implications.

Finally, the emergence of long-acting injectable PrEP (cabotegravir, administered every two months) and the dapivirine vaginal ring as alternatives to daily oral PrEP may help address some of the barriers to continuation identified in this study [verify and cite: Landovitz et al. 2021; Baeten et al. 2021]. By reducing the burden of daily pill-taking, these newer modalities may improve continuation rates among key populations who struggle with the daily oral regimen.

Building Data Science Capacity for Global Health

The third study demonstrated that cohort-based online training can engage health professionals in data science skill-building, achieving completion rates substantially higher than typical MOOCs. Several aspects of the findings deserve further discussion.

The strong performance of both asynchronous (video lessons) and synchronous (live workshops) components supports the pedagogical theory underlying the flipped classroom model: that learning is optimized when self-paced content delivery is paired with guided practice and instructor interaction [verify and cite: Bishop & Verleger 2013]. The particularly high value placed on instructor support during live workshops suggests that for programming education, where learners frequently encounter frustrating errors that are difficult to resolve independently, real-time human guidance remains indispensable.

The finding that time constraints and competing work priorities were the primary barriers to completion highlights a fundamental tension in professional education: the working professionals who most need these skills are often those least able to commit sustained time to structured coursework. Potential solutions include more flexible scheduling options, dedicated catch-up weeks within the course calendar, and offering alternative tracks with different intensities (e.g., a slower-paced 16-week option alongside the standard 12-week programme).

The association between full scholarship receipt and lower completion rates is a nuanced finding. While it may partly reflect the resource constraints that qualified scholarship recipients for financial aid in the first place, it also raises questions about how financial investment affects motivation and commitment. This is consistent with loss aversion effects documented in the MOOC literature, where learners who pay for verified certificates complete at substantially higher rates [verify and cite: Ho et al. 2015; Arkes & Blumer 1985]. Programme designs that incorporate even modest financial commitments or achievement-based refund incentives might improve completion among scholarship recipients.

The Graph Courses: Platform Development and Broader Impact

The development of The Graph Courses platform represented a substantial effort, spanning approximately one year of initial development and involving a team of over 10 contributors. The platform now offers a portfolio of courses in R programming, Python programming, generative AI for research productivity, and a French-language R course, serving learners from dozens of countries worldwide. Several courses are offered free of charge as self-paced tracks, including courses developed in collaboration with the World Health Organization on the Harmonized Health Facility Assessment (HHFA) and health inequality monitoring.

Comprehensive course textbooks have been made freely available online. Introduction to Data Analysis with R (datawithr.com) and Introduction to Data Science with Python (pythondatabook.com) serve both as companion materials for enrolled students and as standalone resources for self-directed learners. These open educational resources extend the platform’s reach well beyond its enrolled student population.

The platform has also served as infrastructure for training partnerships with external organizations. In addition to the WHO collaborations mentioned above, a course developed by the University of Oxford on infectious disease modelling is hosted on the platform. In this model, a centralized learning platform provides the technical infrastructure and pedagogical framework while external organizations contribute specialized content, offering an efficient approach to scaling data science training across the global health sector. It reduces the duplication of effort that occurs when individual organizations each build their own training platforms from scratch.

The sustainability model, which combines grant funding (from the Ernst Goehner Foundation, the Global Fund, and others) with course revenue, has allowed the platform to maintain low fees and provide substantial financial aid to learners from lower-resource settings, which is critical for serving the LMIC populations that the platform aims to reach.

Future Perspectives

Several directions for future work emerge from this thesis.

For the HIV cascade studies, further research using more objective measures of HIV disease status (such as CD4 counts, which were unavailable in the current dataset) would strengthen the analysis of factors associated with viral suppression. Qualitative studies with both FSWs and healthcare providers could illuminate the specific mechanisms driving the observed improvements in testing and suppression rates over time, providing actionable insights for programme design. Additionally, extending the analysis to examine outcomes beyond 6-month viral suppression, including long-term retention in care, treatment switching, and mortality, would provide a more complete picture of the care cascade for this population.

For PrEP continuation research, studies that incorporate data on individual-level risk behaviours, reasons for discontinuation, and biological adherence measures (such as drug-level monitoring) would provide much richer context for interpreting continuation patterns. The rollout of long-acting injectable PrEP in Nigeria and similar settings will also require dedicated evaluation to assess whether these newer modalities improve continuation among the KP groups identified as most vulnerable in this study.

For the data science training programme, longitudinal follow-up studies tracking whether course completion translates into sustained R use and improved work outputs in professional practice would strengthen the evidence base for this training model. Controlled study designs comparing cohort-based and purely self-paced approaches remain a valuable future direction. Such a comparison was in fact the original research plan for this thesis component but was later adapted to an observational design as the programme scaled. The platform’s continued expansion to additional languages, topics (such as advanced statistics, machine learning, and causal inference), and the hosting of additional external courses from partner organizations offers the potential to further scale accessible data science training for the global health workforce.

Limitations

Several limitations should be considered when interpreting the findings of this thesis.

For the HIV studies (Papers 1 and 2), the observational design precludes causal inference. Both studies relied on routinely collected programmatic data, which may be subject to measurement error, incomplete recording, and other data quality issues that the study team could not fully assess [verify and cite: Gloyd et al. 2016]. The analyses may have been affected by unmeasured confounding. For example, individual-level socioeconomic status, substance use, and psychosocial factors were not available in the dataset but likely influence both treatment and prevention outcomes. Additionally, the reliance on WHO clinical staging rather than CD4 counts to classify disease severity (in Paper 1) provides a less precise measure of immunological status. Finally, the findings from the Nigerian KP programme may not be directly generalizable to key populations in other settings with different epidemiological, legal, and service delivery contexts.

For the training study (Paper 3), the absence of a comparison group prevents conclusions about whether the cohort-based format itself drives higher completion rates compared to alternative approaches. Survey respondents over-represented course completers, which likely biases satisfaction estimates upward. The study relied on self-reported data subject to recall and social desirability bias. No longitudinal follow-up was conducted to assess long-term skill retention or professional application. Finally, as authors affiliated with the organization that developed and delivers the course, our interpretation may be subject to bias toward favourable findings.

Conclusion

The three studies in this thesis are united by the premise that data-driven decision-making can improve health outcomes for vulnerable populations, but only if both the data and the skills to analyse it are in place. The first two studies demonstrate this premise in action: routine programmatic data, subjected to rigorous analysis, revealed that Nigeria’s KP programme is achieving strong treatment outcomes for FSWs while facing a critical gap in PrEP continuation, particularly among transgender individuals and younger clients. These findings can directly inform programme design, but they required analytical skills that remain scarce in the settings where such programmes operate.

The third study addresses this scarcity. By evaluating an online training programme that teaches health professionals the very skills used to produce the evidence in Papers 1 and 2, it closes the loop: from identifying health challenges through data analysis, to building the workforce that can perform such analysis at scale. The high completion rates and participant satisfaction suggest that cohort-based online training is a viable model for meeting this need.

Ultimately, this thesis argues that strengthening the HIV response for key populations, and improving global health more broadly, depends not only on better interventions and better data, but on expanding who has the ability to turn data into evidence.
