2. Program outline 
	2.1 Training objectives
	

Skilled epidemiologists are the backbone of effective and efficient public health policy. However,
a variety of bottlenecks obstruct the pipelines expected to  produce adequately trained quantitative researchers in LMICs. In the context of growing infectious disease threats, we propose a transformative training and mentorship program on policy-focused infectious disease modelling to support GS LEARN objectives, with the purpose of strengthening pandemic preparedness and response in the Global South. 


Our goals are to:
1. Build and sustain a multidisciplinary learning ecosystem with regional facilitator organisations and participants for training, mentorship, and collaboration in epidemic analytics and response.
2. Develop a sequence of modular training courses on epidemic modelling techniques in R, customized to the needs of target trainees.
3. Recruit and train diverse cohorts of learners from academic, government, and NGO backgrounds to develop expertise in infectious disease modelling, applied statistics, and strategic policy engagement.
4. Strengthen research-to-policy pipelines by creating opportunities for genuine dialogue and mutually beneficial collaborations between modellers, epidemiologists, and policymakers.
5. Evaluate program activities, report findings, and develop protocols to expand the learning ecosystem model to other Global South regions and institutions. 
	2.2 Methodology and activities[f] 
	Pathway to Pandemic Preparedness
We aim to implement a robust training and mentorship program to train participants in infectious disease modelling, with a strong focus on response strategies and policy implications. Our training program is designed to transform beginners into competent modellers through skill-based learning, project-based coursework, intensive mentorship, and practical work experience (Fig. 1, Goals 2-4).

	2.2.1 Training material creation and delivery
	Content:
Our training program is strategically designed to serve two distinct tracks, each aligned with specific target audiences and objectives. Track 1, which follows the four-course sequence, directly supports Goal 2 by providing comprehensive training in infectious disease modelling for academic participants. This track emphasizes foundational and advanced skills in data analysis, statistical modelling, and infectious disease dynamics, preparing participants for impactful roles in public health research. Track 2, designed for policy-makers, aligns with Goal 4 and focuses on equipping decision-makers with the essential knowledge and tools to understand, assess, and apply infectious disease models in policy formulation. 
Both tracks are structured to ensure that participants acquire the necessary expertise to contribute meaningfully to evidence-based public health decision-making, thereby addressing global health challenges effectively.
Four-course Sequence
This training program is designed  to build a comprehensive pathway to pandemic preparedness by equipping participants[g] with essential skills in infectious disease modelling. Beginning with foundational courses in data analysis and modelling, participants will progress through a carefully structured four-course sequence, each with a focus on practical, skill-based learning. To support Goal 2, we propose to administer the following training courses:
Output 2.1: Introduction to R for Public Health
Output 2.2: Introduction to Mathematical Modelling of Infectious Diseases
Output 2.3: Applied Statistics for Epidemiology
Output 2.4: Advanced Mathematical Modelling of Infectious Diseases
Throughout these courses, students will develop technical expertise in data manipulation, statistical analysis, and infectious disease modelling through video-rich lessons, quizzes, and assignments that reinforce key concepts and skills. Each course culminates in a capstone project, allowing students to apply their learning to real-world health challenges. The program emphasizes hands-on experience, intensive mentorship, and collaboration with partner institutions, ensuring that students gain practical knowledge and are prepared to contribute to evidence-based decision-making in public health. This structured approach ensures graduates are well-equipped to tackle global health challenges. 
The following four-course sequence outlines the detailed curriculum designed to guide students from foundational concepts to advanced modelling techniques. For a detailed breakdown of the lessons and cohort schedule example for the four-course sequence, please refer to Annexes 2.1 and 2.2.
Course 1: Introduction to R for Public Health
Overview
This course offers an introduction to foundations for the R programming language, focusing on its application in public health data analysis. Students will explore essential concepts of R, progressions from basic syntax to an overview of widely used packages for data manipulation and visualization. Course modules involve a modem R curriculum, utilizing libraries like dplyr, ggplot2, and Rmarkdown to equip students to effectively communicate research findings through visual data representation and online publication.
 Learning Outcomes
At the end of the course, learners will be able to handle a wide spectrum of basic and intermediate data analysis tasks using R, including:
* Installing and utilizing R & RStudio
* How to read and write a programming script in R
* How to use and navigate Rmarkdown scripts and RStudio projects
* Importing datasets in R and performing exploratory analysis
* Data manipulation through filtering, summarizing, transforming, joining, and pivoting
* Visualizing data using the ggplot2 package
* Publishing various output formats including reports, websites, and interactive dashboards
* Version control and collaboration using Git and GitHub
* Leveraging ChatGPT and other AI tools as a coding assistant
* Course 2: Introduction to Mathematical Modelling of Infectious Diseases
Overview
This course offers a hands-on introduction to mathematical modeling of infectious diseases. Students will learn to construct models for specific research or policy questions, develop and implement these models using R, and analyze the resulting short- and long-term dynamics. The course will also cover essential techniques for effectively communicating  the modeling process and outcomes to audiences ranging from experts to non-specialists.
 Learning Outcomes
At the end of the course, students will be able to:
* Write a simple mathematical model that is appropriate for a specific infectious disease and related research/policy question
* Explain how critical transmission related parameters such as the reproduction number affect infectious disease dynamics
* Critically examine the short- and long-term dynamics of a model
* Compare and contrast different model outputs to evaluate the effectiveness of different public health interventions

Training for Policy-Makers
Our Training for Policy-Makers course  is designed to provide public health decision-makers with a comprehensive introduction to infectious disease transmission models. This track begins with foundational concepts in epidemiology and modelling, progressing through an exploration of model types, structure, and function. Participants will gain the skills to critically assess the relevance and reliability of models, enabling them to make informed decisions based on model outputs. The course incorporates real-world case studies, demonstrating how models inform policy decisions and addressing key uncertainties in their application. To support Goal 4, we propose to administer the training course with the following overview and learning outcomes:
Overview
This course offers a comprehensive introduction to infectious disease transmission models for public health decision-makers. No prior experience or background in modeling is required. Students will learn the basics of model types, structure, and function, along with methods to assess their relevance and reliability. Through case studies, the course also covers how models inform policy decisions and highlights key uncertainties in their application. 
Learning Outcomes
At the end of the course, students will be able to:
* Understand the basics of infections disease epidemiology and transmission and key components of infectious disease models
* Formulate questions that can be answered by infectious disease transmission models and types of models used to answer each type of question
* Describe how to assess whether or not a model is “good” or useful for informing policy
* Identify the most important considerations for making decisions based on infectious disease transmission models

	2.3 Monitoring and evaluation[p]
	Quality Assurance Throughout the Program


A systematic monitoring process will track program implementation across all training phases. Before launching each course, expert panels will review and validate course content, learning objectives, and assessment tools through multiple rounds of feedback. During delivery, regular collection of both quantitative and qualitative data will enable continuous quality improvement.


For the four-course sequence, weekly monitoring will include tracking attendance, completion rates of pre-work assignments, and participation levels in workshops and help sessions. Academic advisors will conduct monthly one-on-one check-ins with participants to identify learning barriers and provide targeted support. Course coordinators will hold bi-weekly meetings to discuss participant progress and address emerging challenges.


For the research fellowship component, monthly reports from both fellows and mentors will document progress on research projects, skills development, and any implementation challenges. Regular video conferences between program leadership and host institutions will ensure alignment of fellowship experiences with program goals.


Evaluation Framework


The evaluation strategy employs the four-level Kirkpatrick model to assess program effectiveness:
  



Level 1 (Reaction) measures participant satisfaction and engagement through detailed feedback surveys after each course module and final project. Key metrics include satisfaction with course content, teaching methods, mentorship quality, and program logistics.


Level 2 (Learning) assesses knowledge and skill acquisition through pre/post competency assessments for each course. These evaluations will test both theoretical understanding and practical application of epidemiological modeling concepts. Fellowship learning will be evaluated through research project deliverables and mentor assessments.


Level 3 (Behavior) tracks how participants apply their training in their professional roles. Follow-up surveys at 6 and 12 months post-program will document implementation of modeling skills, research outputs, and policy engagement activities. Case studies will highlight examples of successful knowledge translation.


Level 4 (Results) examines broader program impact through multiple indicators: publications in peer-reviewed journals, presentations at scientific conferences, successful grant applications, career advancement, and contributions to policy decisions. A particular focus will be placed on tracking South-South collaborations catalyzed through the training network.


	





3. Experience / knowledge
	3.1 Modelling
	Provide key information on the organisation and description of organisation modelling expertise


The Infectious Diseases and Mathematical Modelling (IDMM)  division of the University of Geneva's Institute of Global Health conducts research in disease modeling to inform health policy, focusing on endemic diseases in low- and middle-income countries. Led by Professor Olivia Keiser, who has 24 years of experience in epidemiological research, the division includes twelve staff researchers based at UNIGE and engages with an extended network of over 100 collaborators, including public health specialists and graduate students worldwide.
The division conducts policy-relevant modeling research on endemic diseases including HIV, tuberculosis, and viral hepatitis, informing both national policies and WHO recommendations (Keebler et al., 2014). The group’s modeling work has advanced understanding of critical areas including viral load monitoring strategies in adults (Estill et al., 2012) and children (Salazar-Vizcaya et al., 2014), the effectiveness of patient tracing systems (Estill et al., 2014), and approaches to prevent mother-to-child transmission (Tweya et al., 2016). During the COVID-19 pandemic, the team supported the WHO Regional Office for Africa (WHO AFRO), managing emergency information across the African region and developing evidence-based response strategies.
IDMM employs diverse methodological approaches including disease progression simulations, dynamic transmission models, and agent-based modeling. The division uses specialized tools such as the TIME Impact model and Spectrum software, while contributing to open-source development through projects like the R package 'gems' (Blaser et al., 2015) for flexible disease progression modeling.


The African Population and Health Research Center (APHRC) in Nairobi is a research-to-policy institution focused on generating evidence, strengthening research capacity, and engaging policymakers to inform action on health and development across sub-Saharan Africa. Over the last two decades, APHRC has implemented over 100 research and policy projects in more than 35 countries. 
APHRC's modeling team has recently applied agent-based modeling (ABM) to study family planning interventions in Nairobi's urban slums, capturing the dynamics of fertility preferences, contraceptive use, and pregnancy-related outcomes. The group’s research on infectious diseases, including HIV, has informed policy by modeling intervention strategies to reduce disease burden.
APHRC collaborates with numerous institutions across Africa, including 19 universities and research institutes, to develop evidence-based public health interventions. The Center leads several major initiatives: the Sida-funded Joint Programming Initiative on Antimicrobial Resistance (JPIAMR) Partnership, the WHO-funded Regional Sexual and Reproductive Health and Rights (SRHR) Implementation Research Hub, and the Consortium for Advanced Research Training in Africa (CARTA), and Capacity Development in Applied Epidemiology (CDAE) program. Additional programs include the Countdown to 2030 Fellowship Program (CD2030) and the Catalyze Impact Initiative, large projects that strengthen research capacity and inform context-specific health interventions across the continent.


Fundação Getúlio Vargas School of Applied Mathematics (FGV-EMAp) in Rio de Janeiro brings expertise in mathematical, statistical, and computational modeling of epidemics to inform national policy, focusing on emerging arbovirus diseases. Led by Professor Favio Coelho, who has over 25 years of experience in infectious disease modeling, the research group has pioneered work in vector-borne disease epidemiology. Key projects include Infodengue, an early-warning system monitoring arboviral diseases across more than 5,300 Brazilian municipalities, and Mosqlimate, a web-based platform integrating epidemiological, climate, and demographic data for arbovirus forecasting.
The group develops advanced modeling techniques to improve disease prediction and surveillance. This includes novel methods for estimating attack ratios in dengue transmission and approaches for connecting models with real-world data through parameter estimation (e.g., Coelho et al., 2011). FGV-EMap’s research also incorporates external factors like climate and social status (e.g., Coelho et al., 2020) and employs continuous disease surveillance to identify long-term risk trends (e.g., Almeida et al., 2023; Codeço et al., 2022).
